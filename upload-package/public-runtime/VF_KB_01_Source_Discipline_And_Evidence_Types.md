## Source quality checklist

For each meaningful source, ask:

- Who created it?
- What is the publication date or effective date?
- Is it primary, secondary, or tertiary?
- What evidence does it provide?
- Does it link to original data, documents, or methods?
- Does it have incentives that may bias presentation?
- Is it current enough for the claim?
- Does it directly support the claim or only something adjacent?
- Does another independent source confirm or challenge it?

## Primary source examples

Primary sources include:

- Statutes, regulations, court opinions, agency guidance, and official notices.
- Original research papers, datasets, and methods appendices.
- Company filings, official product pages, release notes, and technical documentation.
- Direct transcripts, recordings, speeches, interviews, and statements.
- Public records and official databases.

Primary does not always mean correct or complete. It means closer to the origin of the claim.

## Secondary source examples

Secondary sources include:

- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses.
- Standards bodies and expert consensus reports.
- Reputable news analysis with named sources.
- Academic review articles.
- Government or institutional summaries of primary data.

Secondary sources are useful for context and synthesis, especially when they are transparent about methods.

## Weak source warning signs

Treat a source cautiously when it:

- Has no date.
- Has no author or organization.
- Does not link to original evidence.
- Uses vague claims such as "studies show" or "experts agree."
- Makes a strong claim from a single anecdote.
- Uses screenshots as proof without provenance.
- Is mainly selling something.
- Repeats another article without independent reporting.
- Hides methodology for rankings, ratings, or statistics.
- Confuses correlation with causation.

## Claim-to-evidence fit

Match evidence strength to claim strength:

- A quote claim needs the original quote, transcript, recording, or document.
- A legal claim needs jurisdiction, effective date, and authoritative legal source.
- A medical claim needs current expert-body guidance or strong clinical evidence.
- A product claim needs official documentation, release notes, specs, or direct testing.
- A price or availability claim needs current seller, provider, or market data.
- A causal claim needs stronger evidence than timing or correlation.
- A superlative claim needs a defined comparison set and method.

## Common distortion patterns

Watch for:

- Missing denominator: a number is given without the total.
- Base-rate neglect: risk is described without normal comparison.
- Cherry-picked date range: the conclusion depends on a convenient start or end date.
- Scope shift: evidence supports a narrower claim than the headline.
- Category error: sources compare unlike things.
- Outdated truth: a claim was once true but may no longer be.
- Quote laundering: a paraphrase becomes a quotation.
- Circular sourcing: multiple pages cite one another rather than an original source.

## Source summaries

When summarizing sources, include:

- Source name.
- Date.
- Source type.
- What it supports.
- What it does not prove.
- Any important limitation.

Do not quote long passages. Use short excerpts only when exact wording matters.
